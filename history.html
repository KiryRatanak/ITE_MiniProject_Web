Khmer History
Through the Ages

From the early river kingdoms of Funan to the digital age of modern Cambodia — a scholarly yet accessible journey through twelve centuries of Khmer civilization.

JUMP TO →
1st – 8th Century
I

Pre-Angkor Period

សម័យមុនអង្គរ

The Funan Kingdom — Southeast Asia's First Major State

The Funan kingdom (1st–6th century AD) emerged at the mouth of the Mekong River delta, in what is now southern Vietnam and Cambodia. Chinese chronicles describe it as a powerful maritime state that controlled trade routes between India and China. The Funanese built the first hydraulic infrastructure in the region, using canals to drain the vast lowland plains for rice cultivation.

Indian cultural influence was profound — Sanskrit became the language of the court and inscriptions, Brahminism and later Hinduism shaped religious life, and Indian architectural forms influenced temple design. The famous Chinese envoy Kang Tai visited in the 3rd century and reported a kingdom of sophisticated governance, with a court that rivaled those of China.

The kings of Funan were called "mountain kings" — they ruled not just people, but the monsoon itself.

The Chenla Period — Rise of the Khmers

By the late 6th century, the Funan state collapsed and was replaced by Chenla, a more distinctly Khmer-speaking polity. Chinese records refer to two states: Land Chenla (in the Cambodian interior) and Water Chenla (near the coast). The Chenla inscriptions, written in Old Khmer, are among the earliest evidence of the Khmer language and represent a crucial moment in Cambodian national identity.

The inscription at Angkor Borei from 611 AD is considered the oldest dated text in the Khmer language. These stone inscriptions reveal a society with complex land tenure, royal rituals, and temple-based religious communities. The stage was being set for the great empire to come.

Key Facts
  • ▸Capital: Vyadhapura (Funan), then Sambor Prei Kuk (Chenla)
  • ▸Religion: Hinduism (Shaivism), then early Buddhism
  • ▸Oldest inscription: 611 AD, Angkor Borei
  • ▸Trade partners: India, China, Rome
  • ▸Key ruler: Rudravarman I of Funan

802 – 1431 AD
II

The Angkor Empire

សម័យអង្គរ

Jayavarman II (802–835)

Founded the Khmer Empire by performing the devaraja ritual on Phnom Kulen in 802 AD, establishing the concept of the god-king that would define Angkor for six centuries. He broke free from Javanese influence and unified the Khmer-speaking territories.

Suryavarman II (1113–1150)

Builder of Angkor Wat, the largest religious monument in the world. He extended the empire to its greatest geographic reach, conquering territories in modern-day Thailand, Laos, and Vietnam. His funerary temple, dedicated to Vishnu, took 30 years to build.

Jayavarman VII (1181–1218)

The greatest of all Khmer kings. After repelling a Cham invasion, he converted to Mahayana Buddhism and built Angkor Thom, the Bayon temple with its iconic smiling faces, Ta Prohm, Preah Khan, and 102 hospitals across the empire.

The Hydraulic Empire

Angkor's power rested on an astonishing feat of engineering: a system of reservoirs (barays), canals, and distribution channels that covered over 1,000 square kilometers. The West Baray alone measures 8km × 2.1km and held 53 million cubic meters of water. This system allowed three rice harvests per year, feeding a population estimated at 750,000 to 1 million people — making Angkor the largest pre-industrial city in the world.

Recent LiDAR surveys by the Greater Angkor Project have revealed that the urban footprint of Angkor extended across 3,000 square kilometers — larger than modern-day Los Angeles. This "low-density agrarian urbanism" was unique in the premodern world.

Decline of the Empire

Historians debate the causes of Angkor's decline. Contributing factors included repeated Thai (Ayutthaya) attacks, overextension of the hydraulic system, climate-driven droughts in the 14th–15th centuries (confirmed by tree ring analysis), elite religious fragmentation, and shifts in trade routes to maritime networks that bypassed inland Angkor.

In 1431, the Thai kingdom of Ayutthaya sacked Angkor. The Khmer court relocated to Phnom Penh, then Lovek, then Udong, beginning the Post-Angkor era. Angkor Wat itself was never abandoned — Buddhist monks maintained it continuously, which helped preserve it.

1431 – 1863
III

Post-Angkor Period

សម័យក្រោយអង្គរ

After the fall of Angkor, Cambodia entered a period of political fragmentation and external pressure. The kingdom shifted its capital multiple times — from Phnom Penh to Lovek (1529) and then Udong (1618). The "Dark Ages" of Cambodian history saw the kingdom squeezed between the expanding Thai kingdoms to the west and Vietnamese expansion to the east.

Despite political weakness, Theravada Buddhism flourished as the dominant religion, replacing both Hinduism and Mahayana Buddhism. Cambodian literature, dance, and the arts developed distinctive forms during this period. The Reamker (Cambodian version of the Ramayana) became a cornerstone of Khmer cultural identity.

By the 19th century, Cambodia had lost vast territories to Thailand (including Battambang and Siem Reap) and Vietnam (the Mekong Delta, once called Kampuchea Krom). Facing extinction as a state, King Norodom signed a protectorate agreement with France in 1863.

Key Development

Lovek Falls (1594)

The Thai sack of Lovek was so devastating that legends say the Khmers lost their sacred texts — and their golden age — in one night.

Cultural Legacy

Reamker Epic

The Khmer adaptation of the Ramayana, composed in this era, remains the foundation of classical Cambodian dance and theatre.

1863 – 1953
IV

French Protectorate

សម័យអាណានិគម

France's protectorate over Cambodia (1863–1953) had contradictory effects. On one hand, French colonial administrators reformed the traditional administrative system, introduced Western education and infrastructure, and — crucially — mapped, restored, and publicized Angkor Wat to the world. The École française d'Extrême-Orient (EFEO) conducted systematic archaeological research that transformed understanding of Khmer civilization.

On the other hand, France maintained nominal Khmer royalty as figureheads while extracting rice, rubber, and taxes. In 1907, France negotiated the return of Siem Reap and Battambang from Thailand — a significant territorial gain for Cambodia. The colonial period also gave rise to a French-educated Cambodian elite who would later lead the independence movement — and, tragically, the Khmer Rouge.

Rediscovery of Angkor

Henri Mouhot's 1860 account of Angkor "rediscovered" the site for Western audiences — though Cambodians had never forgotten it. French restoration work from 1907–1970 cleared vegetation, stabilized structures, and created the first systematic records of Angkor's hundreds of temples.

The Independence Movement

Young King Norodom Sihanouk proved a masterful political actor, using public pressure, diplomacy, and his own abdication as leverage to force France to grant Cambodia independence on November 9, 1953 — peacefully, without armed conflict.

1953 – 1979
V

Independence & Tragedy

ឯករាជ្យ និងមហន្តរាយ

The Sihanouk era (1953–1970) was a golden age of Cambodian arts, cinema, architecture, and relative prosperity. Phnom Penh was called the "Pearl of Asia." However, Cold War entanglements — US bombing of Communist Vietnamese supply routes through Cambodia, Sihanouk's tightrope neutrality — destabilized the country. The US-backed coup of 1970 brought General Lon Nol to power.

The Khmer Rouge (1975–1979)

April 17, 1975: the Khmer Rouge, led by Pol Pot, seized Phnom Penh and evacuated the entire population of the capital within days. Their utopian agrarian revolution — "Year Zero" — resulted in the death of an estimated 1.7 to 2.5 million people, nearly a quarter of Cambodia's population, through execution, starvation, overwork, and disease.

Liberation

On January 7, 1979, Vietnamese forces entered Phnom Penh and ended the Khmer Rouge regime. The country was in ruins — with its educated class decimated, infrastructure destroyed, and a generation of children who had known nothing but terror. Reconstruction would take decades.

1979 – Present
VI

Modern Cambodia

ព្រះរាជាណាចក្រកម្ពុជា

1991–1993

Paris Peace Accords

The 1991 Paris Peace Agreements ended the civil war. UN peacekeepers (UNTAC) oversaw the 1993 elections, resulting in a coalition government. Norodom Sihanouk returned as king. Cambodia began the long road to democracy and economic recovery.

2000s–2010s

Economic Growth

Cambodia emerged as one of Asia's fastest-growing economies, driven by garment manufacturing, tourism, and construction. Phnom Penh transformed into a modern city. International tourist arrivals at Angkor Wat surpassed 2 million annually by 2010.

Today

A Nation Remembering

Today's Cambodia grapples with its history while building its future. The ECCC (Extraordinary Chambers in the Courts of Cambodia) tried surviving Khmer Rouge leaders. A young generation is rediscovering traditional arts, language, and pride in Khmer identity.
